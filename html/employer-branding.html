What to Know Before You Build Your Employer Brand

What is employer branding?

+

Employer branding is how current and potential employees perceive your company as a workplace. It shapes whether people want to join, stay, grow, and advocate for the organisation.

Why does employer branding matter?

+

It helps the right talent understand what your company stands for, what working there feels like, and why they should consider building a future with you.

What is an Employee Value Proposition?

+

An Employee Value Proposition, or EVP, is the promise your organisation makes to its people. It defines what employees receive, experience, and believe in exchange for their talent, effort, and commitment.

How is employer branding different from recruitment marketing?

+

Employer branding builds long-term workplace perception. Recruitment marketing turns that perception into interest, applications, and candidate journeys for specific hiring needs.

What kind of content works for employer branding?

+

Employee stories, leadership narratives, culture moments, behind-the-scenes content, workplace campaigns, career-site messaging, and internal communication all help make the employer brand visible.

How do you ensure internal alignment?

+

We build messaging frameworks, tone guidance, content systems, and internal communication tools so leadership, HR, communications, and employees are aligned around the same employer story.

How is employer branding success measured?

+

Success can be measured through employer brand perception, application quality, offer acceptance, employee advocacy, engagement on career platforms, internal participation, and retention indicators.

Who should be involved from our side?

+

HR, talent acquisition, corporate communications, leadership, marketing, and selected employee voices should usually be involved because employer branding needs both strategy and lived reality.

How do we know if we need employer branding?

+

You need it when talent does not understand why your company is worth joining, when your hiring communication feels generic, or when your internal culture is stronger than the story the outside world sees.
